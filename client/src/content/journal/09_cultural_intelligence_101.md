# Cultural Intelligence 101: How To Launch A Brand Movement in Nigeria

*By Jesse Dan-Yusuf.*

---

You can spot the imported playbook from three aisles away. The typography is from a Medium article someone read in 2022, the palette is whatever Figma was trending that quarter, and the launch copy reads like it was translated from an accelerator deck in San Francisco. The founder insists the brand is "for Nigerians," yet nothing in it was built with any real sense of what Nigerians believe, mock, love, or roll their eyes at by the second week.

This is the single most expensive mistake I watch founders make. They raise money, hire consultants, buy a beautiful identity system, and then wonder why the launch landed with the thud of a generator that refuses to catch.

Brand movements do not start with design. They start with listening. Specifically, they start with listening to the specific market you claim to be building for, closely enough that you notice the jokes, the suspicions, the status games, and the shortcuts people take when nobody is watching. That is cultural intelligence. Everything else is decoration.

The brands that actually move in Nigeria tend to share one thing, which is that the people who built them were paying attention to Nigeria. Not the Nigeria of investor memos. The actual one. The one where trust is scarce and your grandmother is still the most credible marketing channel in the country. The one where a brand that takes itself too seriously will be ridiculed by Thursday, and a brand that takes itself not seriously enough will be dismissed as another one of those. The balance is narrower than most founders realise until they have already missed it.

If I had to offer any genuinely useful principle for building a movement here, it would be this. Start by naming what the brand refuses. Nigerians have an unusually sharp eye for pretence, and if your brand cannot say clearly what it is not willing to be, it will get tested until it tells you. Pretending to be a luxury brand while running a factory-seconds operation in Ogba is the kind of gap that gets exposed on TikTok within a fortnight. The brands that hold their shape are the ones whose founders decided early which shortcuts they were not going to take, and meant it.

The second thing is a slower truth, which is that community precedes campaign. A launch in Nigeria is a stress test of the relationships you built before the product existed. If you arrive cold with a billboard and a hashtag, the market will treat you the way it treats most strangers, which is with polite distance. If you arrive having spent eighteen months in the group chats, the studios, the salons, the Sunday services, the right listservs, the churches or the mosques, the market makes room for you. Founders skip this step because it cannot be line-itemed on a budget. It is also the step that separates brands that launch from brands that move.

The third is that cultural intelligence is not a research deliverable. You cannot pay a strategy consultant to import it for you, although several will happily sell you the illusion. It lives in the founder and, if you are lucky, in the creative partners you choose to build with. If the people shaping your brand have never had the experience of being ignored at a Lagos bank counter, or haggled over pepper in Wuse market, or watched an entire dinner party pivot in tone the moment an elder walks in, you are going to miss texture your audience can feel from a kilometre away. Hire the people who have the texture. Pay them well. Argue with them often.

The last thing I will say is that a movement in Nigeria looks like a brand that people defend when you are not in the room. Everything else, including the identity, the website, the launch event, the capsule collection, the founder interview, is in service of earning that defence. The brands that do not earn it die quietly, which is its own kind of Nigerian tradition. The ones that earn it become the story people tell about themselves for years.

That is where the work is.
