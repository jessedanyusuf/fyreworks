# Why Your Brand Needs A Creative Director

*By Jesse Dan-Yusuf.*

---

I get this question almost every week, in some form or another. It usually arrives politely, sometimes from founders I am already in conversation with, sometimes from people who stumbled onto the studio and want to understand what they are looking at. The question is always the same underneath the phrasing. Why do I need you when I can get a logo on Fiverr for forty dollars and a freelancer on Upwork to run my socials for five hundred a month?

It is a fair question. I want to answer it honestly, because the honest answer is more interesting than the defensive one most studios would give you.

You can absolutely get a logo on Fiverr. You can hire a freelancer for your socials. You can find a decent web developer for a good price on any of the usual platforms. The work will probably be fine. For a small business that only needs a few assets and has no ambition beyond operational marketing, this is often a reasonable choice. I am not here to tell you that every founder needs a creative director, because not every founder does.

What I want to talk about is what happens when your ambition is larger than "fine." Because this is where the Fiverr-and-freelancer approach quietly starts to cost you more than it saves you, and most founders do not notice until a year or two later when they are trying to figure out why their brand still does not feel like anything.

Here is the part that never gets said out loud. A logo made in isolation, by a designer who has no relationship to the rest of your brand, is just a shape. It might be a pretty shape. It is not yet a brand. The social content made by a freelancer who was briefed on a single Google Doc is just posts. The website built by the developer who was never told what the brand is supposed to mean is just a page. Each of these things can be competent on its own and still add up to nothing, because there is nobody in the middle deciding what they are all supposed to say together.

That is the job of a creative director. I am not the person who makes every asset. I am the person who decides what they are all supposed to mean, and then makes sure they mean it. The logo, the site, the photography, the tone of voice in the emails, the way the brand shows up at events, the packaging, the sound of the ad, the typography on the invoice. Someone has to hold the line across all of that, or the brand drifts into whatever each individual vendor happened to feel like delivering that week.

Most founders discover this the slow, expensive way. They hire Fiverr for the logo. They hire a freelancer for the socials. They hire another freelancer for the website. Each of these people does their job reasonably well. The logo looks fine. The socials post on schedule. The website launches. Then the founder realises that the logo and the socials do not feel like the same brand. The website speaks in a voice the founder does not actually use. The packaging, when it arrives, looks like it was designed for a different company. The brand has become a collection of assets with no argument behind them, and no individual vendor is wrong, because no individual vendor was ever responsible for the whole.

By the time you notice this, you have usually spent more money fixing it than you would have spent directing it from the start. I have watched founders pay three times for the same brand. Once for the original uncoordinated work, once for the rework to fix the inconsistencies, and once for the real brand build that should have happened in the first place. A creative director is the person who prevents that cycle.

There is also a more subtle cost I should name, because it is the one founders underestimate. When your brand is a collection of assets rather than a coherent argument, your customers cannot tell what you actually stand for. They remember you as "that company with the nice logo" or "the one with the clean website," but they do not remember you as a thing they believe in. Your brand has no weight. It has no posture. It cannot defend itself against a competitor with a sharper point of view, because it does not have a point of view of its own. This does not show up on a balance sheet. It shows up in the slowness of your growth, the thinness of your repeat business, and the fact that your customers can be wooed away by the next brand that walks in looking more considered.

So the real question is not whether you can find cheaper help. You can. The real question is what your ambition is and whether your brand, as it currently exists, is capable of carrying it. If you are running a small operation that needs functional marketing, please do not hire a creative director. Hire good freelancers and get on with it. But if you are building something that needs to mean something over time, something that has to hold its shape in front of customers, investors, partners, and competitors, then you need somebody in the middle deciding what it all adds up to. That is what a creative director does. The assets come out of that decision. The decision comes first.

I will say one last thing, because it is the part that feels too direct to put in a pitch and so I will put it here instead. A lot of founders discover halfway through building their company that they underinvested in brand because brand felt like the soft, nice-to-have category. Then they spend the next two years trying to catch up, usually while watching a competitor with a clearer brand outrun them on the same product. I have seen this enough times that I now treat it as the single most predictable mistake in the founder playbook. You can still have cheap logos and freelance social posts. Just do not pretend that is a brand. And do not be surprised when it does not behave like one.
