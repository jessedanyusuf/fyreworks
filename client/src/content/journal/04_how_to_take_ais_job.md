# How To Take AI's Job

*By Jesse Dan-Yusuf.*

---

Everyone's worried about AI taking their job. I think we're asking the wrong question.

The right question is: how do you take AI's job?

Not by competing with it on execution. You'll lose. AI is faster, cheaper, and doesn't need sleep. If your value is tied to output speed or volume, you're done.

AI is really good at one thing: generating options based on patterns it's seen before. It's terrible at everything else, and "everything else" is where the real value is.

Do what it can't.

Exercise judgment. AI can give you ten options. You need to pick the right one. This is the skill that matters now. Discernment over generation. The ability to look at something and know, with confidence, whether it works or doesn't. Whether it's true to the vision or just well-executed noise.

Judgment is pattern recognition plus conviction. It's seeing what's missing, what's wrong, or what's almost-but-not-quite-there before anyone else does. It's saying "this, not that" and being right about it. AI can't do this. It can only tell you what's statistically probable. You have to tell it what's right.

Understand context. AI knows patterns. You understand people. A brand that works in New York might fall flat in Abuja. A campaign that resonates with Gen Z might alienate your actual audience. A design trend that's hot right now might be dead in six months.

AI doesn't know this. It can't read a room. It can't sense a cultural moment. It doesn't understand the difference between what's popular and what's relevant.

Context is everything, and context requires cultural intelligence. The ability to understand not just what people say, but what they mean. Not just what they like, but what they believe. That's human work, and it's irreplaceable.

Build taste. Taste is the new economy, and taste isn't something you prompt into existence. It's something you develop.

Look at thousands of things. Pay attention to what works and what doesn't. Study the why behind good work. Learn to see the difference between decoration and direction, between clever and meaningful, between trendy and timeless.

Taste is curation. It's the ability to edit, refine, and elevate. It's knowing what to add, what to remove, and when to leave something alone. AI generates while you curate, and curation is what people will pay for.

Hold a point of view. AI is a mirror. It reflects back what it's been trained on, the average of everything it's seen. That means it's safe. Predictable. Generic. You can choose something different.

The brands that break through aren't the ones that look like everyone else. They're the ones with conviction. The ones that say "this is what we believe" and mean it.

That requires a point of view. A creative thesis. A position you're willing to defend even when it's easier to follow trends or play it safe.

AI can help you execute that vision. The vision itself comes from you.

Make connections AI can't see. AI is trained on what's already been done. It's really good at combining existing ideas in new ways, but it can't make the leap to something truly original because originality requires seeing connections that don't exist yet.

That's human creativity. The ability to pull from disparate influences (culture, psychology, history, art, music, politics) and synthesize something new. To see parallels no one else sees. To make metaphors that land.

AI remixes while you create.

Build relationships. AI can write an email. Trust requires human connection. The work that matters, the work that lasts, is relational. It's about understanding what a founder is trying to build, not just what they're asking for. It's about earning the right to challenge their assumptions, push back on bad ideas, and advocate for what's right even when it's uncomfortable.

That's not a prompt. That's a relationship, and relationships are still the foundation of good creative work.

The jobs that survive and thrive in the AI era aren't the ones that compete with machines. They're the ones that do what machines can't: judgment, context, taste, conviction, connection. These are the skills worth developing. Not because AI will never get there (it might), but because right now, and for the foreseeable future, this is where the value is.

At FYREWORKS, we use AI to handle the execution so we can focus on the direction. The strategy. The judgment calls. The cultural intelligence. The creative decisions that can't be automated.

We're not worried about AI taking our job. We're focused on doing the work AI can't touch.

If you're a visionary building something that matters, that's the kind of partner you need.
