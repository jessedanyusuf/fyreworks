# Luxury, The Most Abused Word In Branding Right Now

*By Jesse Dan-Yusuf.*

---

Somewhere in Lekki right now, a brand is calling itself luxury because the founder chose a serif typeface and a gold foil. Somewhere in Abuja, a tailor is calling his atelier luxury because the shirts cost four hundred thousand naira. Somewhere on Instagram, a candle is luxury because the wax is soy, the jar has a minimal label, and the caption includes the word "elevated." None of these things are luxury. Most of them are just expensive, and even that is a generous reading.

I love us, but we have to talk.

The word luxury has taken a beating in the Nigerian market over the last five years, mostly because we keep using it to mean "costs more than my friend thinks it should." A ten thousand naira scented candle does not become luxury because it is ten thousand naira. It becomes a ten thousand naira candle, and whether it is luxury depends on almost everything about it except the price tag.

Luxury, as an actual idea, is about restraint more than it is about excess. The best luxury brands in the world are the ones you could walk past without noticing if you did not already know what you were looking at. Hermès does not need to tell you it is Hermès. Loro Piana does not announce itself. Bottega Veneta went through an entire phase of removing its logo from everything it made. Restraint is expensive, because restraint requires discipline, and discipline is harder to buy than fabric.

The Nigerian use of luxury tends to do the opposite. It shouts. Gold on gold. Marble everywhere. The word "luxury" literally on the storefront, in case anyone missed the marble. Founders conflate maximalism with premium, forgetting that maximalism is its own aesthetic with its own codes, and is almost never what the global luxury market means by the term. What we are really selling in most cases is aspiration, which is a legitimate thing to sell, but aspiration and luxury are not the same product.

In Nigeria, we've trained ourselves to look for the spectacle and miss the consistency. We're impressed by the grand opening, the celebrity endorsement, the expensive finishes. We don't notice that the service is inconsistent, the quality varies batch to batch, and the brand experience falls apart the moment you're past the Instagram-worthy entrance.

That's expensive pretending to be luxury.

Luxury is earned through craft, not claimed through adjectives. You can't become a luxury brand by calling yourself one. You become luxury by obsessing over your craft until people recognize the difference without you having to announce it.

The best craftspeople in Nigeria (and there are many) rarely call themselves luxury. They just do exceptional work. Their clients call them luxury. Their peers recognize it. The market rewards it. But they don't lead with the word because the work speaks.

When a brand leads with "luxury" in Nigeria, it's usually because the work can't speak for itself yet. So they borrow the word to signal what they hope people will believe.

Luxury is also about time, which is the one thing almost no new Nigerian brand has. A luxury brand is one that has survived its own trends, ridden out at least one generation of consumers, and emerged with its story intact. A brand that launched fourteen months ago on a Shopify template is aspirational. It might be beautiful. It might even deserve its price tag. It is not yet luxury, because nothing becomes luxury in fourteen months. Not fashion, not spirits, not real estate, not hospitality. Time is the price luxury pays to become what it is.

The other thing luxury is, and this is the part that makes most Nigerian founders uncomfortable, is exclusive. Really exclusive. Most of the brands using the word here would rather not have to turn customers away, because turning customers away is the opposite of how you scale a business in a market with thin demand. Fair enough. Keep the revenue. But if you cannot or will not say no to a customer, stop calling yourself luxury and just call yourself premium. Premium is honest. Premium says, "this costs more and is better made." Luxury says, "we are choosing not to sell this to most of you." Those are different businesses.

I am being a little sharp because the conversation needs it. The word is being worn out in real time, and every founder who uses it loosely is making it harder for the brands that might eventually earn it. If we keep calling every gold-foiled candle and glass-front boutique a luxury brand, the word will eventually mean what in Nigeria it already half means, which is expensive-ish and trying very hard. That is not a category anyone should be trying to win.

Call it premium. Call it elevated. Call it considered. Save luxury for when you have earned the silence it is supposed to come with.
