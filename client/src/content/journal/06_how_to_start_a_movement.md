# How To Start A Movement

*By Jesse Dan-Yusuf.*

---

Everyone wants to build a movement. Few people understand what that actually means.

A movement goes beyond viral campaigns, trending hashtags, clever manifestos, launch videos, or community Slacks with 500 members.

A movement is what happens when people organize around a shared belief and act on it.

That's it. That's the whole thing.

Everything else (the brand, the strategy, the community, the content) is just infrastructure to support that core idea: belief plus action equals movement.

If you want to start a movement, begin with belief before branding. Most people start with the logo. The name. The colors. The website. That's backwards.

Before you design anything, you need to know what you believe. Not what you want to sell. Not what the market wants to hear. What you actually believe about the world, the thing that's true to you, whether or not anyone agrees.

This is your thesis. Your creative conviction. The idea that everything else will be built around.

Good movements have strong beliefs. Great movements have controversial beliefs, the kind that some people will reject immediately, and others will rally around instantly.

You're not trying to appeal to everyone. You're trying to find the people who believe what you believe.

Make it about more than you. Most founders get stuck because they think the movement is about their product. Their brand. Their vision. The movement needs to be bigger than that.

A movement centers on the people who join it. It's about us, not me. It's about what we believe, what we're building, what we're doing together.

The moment you shift from "look what I made" to "look what we're building together," everything changes. People stop being customers. They become participants. Contributors. Co-creators.

That's the shift that turns a brand into a movement.

Give people something to do. Belief without action remains just an idea people agree with rather than a movement.

Movements move. They require action. Participation. Something tangible people can do to express their belief and be part of the change.

This is where most brands fail. They ask people to buy. Or follow. Or share. These actions feel transactional rather than meaningful.

Real movements give people meaningful ways to participate: contribute ideas and shape what's next. Join conversations and build the culture. Invite others and expand the community. Create content and tell the story. Take a stand and make the belief visible.

When people have something to do, something that actually matters, they stop being an audience. They become a movement.

Build in public. Movements don't happen behind closed doors. They happen in public, where people can see them forming and choose to join.

This means being open about what you're building, why it matters, and what's not working yet. It means sharing the process, not just the polished result. It means inviting people in before everything is perfect.

Most founders wait until they're ready. Until the brand is done. Until the product is perfect. Until they have all the answers.

By then, it's too late. The movement already started without them, or it never started at all.

Building in public is uncomfortable, but it's the only way movements form because movements need momentum, and momentum requires visibility.

Create spaces for connection. Movements don't exist in isolation. They exist in community, spaces where believers can find each other, connect, and build together.

This could be a Discord. A newsletter. A monthly meetup. A Slack group. A physical space. It doesn't matter what the platform is. What matters is that it exists and that it's designed for connection over consumption.

Your job involves creating these spaces, stewarding them, and stepping back rather than controlling them. Let the community develop its own voice, its own culture, its own energy.

The best movements are the ones the founder no longer fully controls because the community has taken ownership.

Be consistent. Movements don't happen overnight. They take time. Repetition. Consistency.

You have to keep showing up. Keep saying the thing you believe. Keep inviting people in. Keep creating opportunities for action.

Most people give up too early. They launch something, don't see immediate traction, and move on to the next idea.

Movements compound slowly. Every person who joins brings others. Every action creates momentum. Every conversation deepens the belief.

You're building a culture rather than a campaign, and culture takes time.

Lead with conviction. Movements follow leaders who believe in something deeply enough to stake their reputation on it.

If you're hedging, if you're trying to appeal to everyone, if you're softening your message to avoid controversy, if you're constantly pivoting based on feedback, you're chasing approval rather than leading a movement.

Movements require conviction. The willingness to say "this is what we believe" and hold that line even when it's uncomfortable. Even when people disagree. Even when it's easier to compromise.

Conviction means clarity rather than arrogance. It's knowing what you stand for and being willing to lose people who don't align.

That's what makes a movement powerful. Not because everyone agrees, but because the people who do agree, believe deeply.

Starting a movement isn't about having the biggest budget, the best design, or the most followers.

It's about having something worth believing in and giving people a way to believe in it together.

If you're building something that matters, you're not just building a brand. You're building a movement.

Every movement starts the same way: with one person willing to say what they believe, and invite others to believe it with them.

Brands come and go. Movements change things.
