# Stop Telling Bad Stories
## Why Your Brand Narrative Isn't Landing

*By Jesse Dan-Yusuf.*

---

Every brand has a story. Most of them are terrible.

Not because the founders can't write. Not because they don't have something interesting to say. But because they're telling the wrong story in the wrong way for the wrong reasons.

I've heard hundreds of brand stories in the last few years. Founder started in their garage. Company was born from a personal struggle. Product came from a gap in the market. Mission is to make X more accessible. Vision is to revolutionize Y.

These stories all sound the same because they're all structured the same way. They follow a template. They hit the expected beats. They're perfectly crafted and completely forgettable.

Your story isn't about you. This is where most brands get it wrong. They think their story is their origin. Their founder's journey. Their mission statement written in narrative form.

Nobody cares where you started. They care where they're going with you.

The best brand stories center on the audience, not the company. They're about the change you're making in the world and the role the audience plays in that change. You're a character in the story, but you're not the main character. The people you serve are.

When you make the story about yourself, you're asking people to care about your journey. When you make it about them, you're inviting them into something bigger.

Stories need conflict, not just context. Most brand stories are all setup and no tension. "We saw a problem. We built a solution. Now we're here to help you."

That's not a story. That's a pitch.

Stories work because they have stakes. Something is at risk. Something needs to change. Someone has to make a difficult choice. The outcome matters.

What's the conflict in your brand's story? What's at stake if you fail? What happens if nothing changes? Why does this matter now rather than five years from now or five years from then?

If your story doesn't have genuine tension, it won't hold attention.

Generic language kills stories. The moment you use words like "innovative," "passionate," "dedicated," or "committed to excellence," you've lost. Not because these things aren't true, but because they're invisible. Everyone says them. They mean nothing.

Good stories use specific language. Concrete details. Real moments. Actual decisions that could have gone differently.

Instead of "we're passionate about sustainability," tell me about the supplier meeting where you walked away from a cheaper option because it violated what you believe. Instead of "we're dedicated to quality," tell me about the batch you destroyed because it didn't meet your standard, even though it would have passed everyone else's.

Specificity is what makes stories believable. Generalities make them forgettable.

Your story needs to evolve. Too many brands tell the same story year after year. The founding story. The mission statement. The vision for the future.

Stories are living things. They grow. They change. They respond to what's happening in the world and what your community needs to hear right now.

If you're telling the same story you told three years ago, you're not paying attention. Your audience has changed. The market has changed. You've changed. Your story should reflect that.

Stop protecting your story and start letting it breathe. Let it evolve with you. Let it take unexpected turns. Let it become something you didn't plan.

Stories need proof, not promises. The weakest brand stories are the ones that live entirely in the future. "We will revolutionize. We're going to change. We aim to transform."

Future tense is fiction until you make it fact. If your entire story is about what you plan to do, you're asking people to believe in potential rather than proof.

The strongest stories show evidence. What have you already done? What decisions have you already made? What have you sacrificed? What have you built? What have you learned?

Your track record is your story. Everything else is marketing.

Make it easy to retell. The brands with the best stories are the ones people can repeat. Not because they're simple, but because they're memorable. They have a core that sticks.

If someone can't explain what your brand is about in two sentences after visiting your website, your story is too complicated. If they can't remember a single detail that makes you different, your story isn't distinct enough.

The test of a good brand story is whether your audience can tell it to someone else without pulling up your website. If they can, you've built something that spreads. If they can't, you're still explaining.

Most brands are telling the story they think they're supposed to tell rather than the story that's actually true. They're following formulas instead of following their conviction. They're trying to sound like everyone else instead of sounding like themselves.

Stop telling bad stories. Start telling true ones. Ones with tension, specificity, and stakes. Ones that center your audience and invite them into something bigger. Ones that evolve as you grow and prove themselves through action.
