# "I Don't Need A Creative Director, I Have ChatGPT"

*By Jesse Dan-Yusuf.*

---

I've heard this three times in the last month. Smart founders, serious about their brands, genuinely convinced that AI has replaced the need for creative direction.

I get it. ChatGPT can write copy. Midjourney can generate visuals. Claude can build you a website. The tools are incredible, and they're only getting better. Why pay someone to do what a machine can do for $20 a month?

AI serves as a tool for execution. Judgment still requires human direction.

AI gives you options but doesn't tell you which one matters. You can prompt ChatGPT to write ten different brand taglines. It'll give you ten grammatically correct, strategically sound options. Some might even be good. Which one is right? Which one feels true to what you're building? Which one will resonate with your audience in six months, not just today? Which one has the legs to become a rallying cry instead of just a clever line? AI can generate options. The discernment comes from you.

AI understands patterns rather than your specific brand. When you use AI to build a brand, you get something that looks like a brand. It has all the parts: logo, colors, fonts, messaging. It might even look polished.

The meaning is missing. AI is trained on millions of brands. It knows what brands generally look like. It doesn't know what your brand should look like, feel like, or stand for.

A creative director does. They take the time to understand your vision, your market, your audience, your moment. They ask questions AI doesn't know to ask. They see what's missing before you do. They make decisions based on context, not just patterns.

AI can't read a room, a culture, or a moment. Let's say you're building a premium brand in Nigeria. You want it to feel sophisticated but not alienating. Global but not generic. Aspirational but rooted.

You prompt AI to generate a visual identity. It gives you something sleek and modern because that's what "premium" looks like in its training data. It doesn't know that your audience reads certain design cues as "trying too hard" or "not for us." It doesn't know the cultural nuances that make a brand feel authentic versus imported.

A creative director knows, because creative direction isn't just design. It's cultural intelligence. It's the ability to read a market, understand an audience, and make decisions that land.

AI serves as your assistant, with you as director. AI is incredible for execution. I use it all the time. It speeds up workflows, generates options, handles repetitive tasks. It's a powerful tool in the hands of someone who knows how to use it.

Tools don't make decisions. They don't have taste. They don't exercise judgment. They don't know when to break the rules or when to hold the line.

That's what a creative director does. We use AI. We use design software. We use our experience, our intuition, our understanding of culture and psychology and narrative. We make choices, not just outputs.

Should you use AI? Absolutely. Should you replace creative direction with it? Only if you're okay with a brand that looks like everyone else's.

AI can't take your vision and turn it into something that moves people. It can't make the call on what matters and what doesn't. It can't look at your brand and say "this is wrong" before you launch it. It can't give you conviction.

Conviction is what separates brands that blend in from brands that break through.

You don't need to choose between AI and creative direction. You need both. AI for speed and scale. Creative direction for discernment and depth. One handles execution. The other handles everything else.

At FYREWORKS, we use AI to move faster so we can spend more time on the work that actually matters. The strategy. The judgment. The cultural intelligence. The creative decisions that make a brand yours, not just another algorithm's interpretation of what a brand should be.

ChatGPT is a great assistant, but it's not your creative director.

—Jesse Dan-Yusuf
